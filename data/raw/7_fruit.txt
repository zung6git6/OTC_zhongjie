<p><i>Rubus</i> subg. <i>Eubatus</i>
</p>
</td></tr>

</tbody></table><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1220493105">html.skin-theme-clientpref-night .mw-parser-output .infobox.biota tr{background:transparent!important}html.skin-theme-clientpref-night .mw-parser-output .infobox.biota img{background:white}@media(prefers-color-scheme:dark){html.skin-theme-clientpref-os .mw-parser-output .infobox.biota tr{background:transparent!important}html.skin-theme-clientpref-os .mw-parser-output .infobox.biota img{background:white}}</style>
<p>The <b>blackberry</b> is an edible fruit produced by many <a href="/wiki/Species" title="Species">species</a> in the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Rubus" title="Rubus">Rubus</a></i> in the <a href="/wiki/Family_(biology)" title="Family (biology)">family</a> <a href="/wiki/Rosaceae" title="Rosaceae">Rosaceae</a>, hybrids among these species within the subgenus <i>Rubus</i>, and hybrids between the subgenera <i>Rubus</i> and <i>Idaeobatus</i>. The taxonomy of blackberries has historically been confused because of <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Apomixis" title="Apomixis">apomixis</a>, so that species have often been grouped together and called <a href="/wiki/Species_aggregate" class="mw-redirect" title="Species aggregate">species aggregates</a>. For example, the entire subgenus <i>Rubus</i> has been called the <i><a href="/wiki/Rubus_fruticosus" title="Rubus fruticosus">Rubus fruticosus</a></i> aggregate, although the species <i>R. fruticosus</i> is considered a synonym of <i><a href="/wiki/Rubus_plicatus" title="Rubus plicatus">R. plicatus</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Jarvis_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Jarvis-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Blackberry fruit production is abundant with annual volumes of 20,000 pounds (9,100&#160;kg) per 1 acre (0.40&#160;ha) possible, making this plant commercially attractive.<sup id="cite_ref-carroll_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carroll-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><i><a href="/wiki/Rubus_armeniacus" title="Rubus armeniacus">Rubus armeniacus</a></i> ("Himalayan" blackberry) is considered a <a href="/wiki/Noxious_weed" title="Noxious weed">noxious weed</a> and <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> in many regions of the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a> of Canada and the United States, where it grows out of control in urban and suburban parks and woodlands.<sup id="cite_ref-iscbc_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iscbc-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-king_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-king-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" />
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>What distinguishes the blackberry from its <a href="/wiki/Raspberry" title="Raspberry">raspberry</a> relatives is whether or not the torus (<a href="/wiki/Receptacle_(botany)" title="Receptacle (botany)">receptacle</a> or stem) "picks with" (i.e., stays with) the fruit. When picking a blackberry fruit, the torus stays with the fruit. With a raspberry, the torus remains on the plant, leaving a hollow core in the raspberry fruit.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<div style="clear:left;" class=""></div>
<p>The term <i><a href="/wiki/Bramble" class="mw-redirect" title="Bramble">bramble</a></i>, a word referring to any impenetrable <a href="/wiki/Thicket" title="Thicket">thicket</a>, has in some circles traditionally been applied specifically to the blackberry or its products,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> though in the United States it applies to all members of the genus <i>Rubus</i>. In the western US, the term <i>caneberry</i> is used to refer to blackberries and raspberries as a group rather than the term bramble. <i><a href="/wiki/Thicket" title="Thicket">Briar</a></i> or <i>brier</i> may be used to refer to the dense vines of the plant, though this name is used for other thorny thickets (such as <i><a href="/wiki/Smilax" title="Smilax">Smilax</a></i>) as well.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2023)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The usually black fruit is not a <a href="/wiki/Berry_(botany)" title="Berry (botany)">berry</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Botanical" class="mw-redirect" title="Botanical">botanical</a> sense, as it is termed botanically as an <a href="/wiki/Aggregate_fruit" title="Aggregate fruit">aggregate fruit</a>, composed of small <a href="/wiki/Drupe" title="Drupe">drupelets</a>. It is a widespread and well-known group of over 375 species, many of which are closely related <a href="/wiki/Apomixis" title="Apomixis">apomictic microspecies</a> <a href="/wiki/Native_plant" class="mw-redirect" title="Native plant">native</a> throughout Europe, northwestern Africa, <a href="/wiki/Temperateness" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperateness">temperate</a> western and central Asia and North and South America.<sup id="cite_ref-rhs_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rhs-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plants">Plants</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plants"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:PRubus_primocanes_and_floricanes.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/PRubus_primocanes_and_floricanes.jpg/220px-PRubus_primocanes_and_floricanes.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/PRubus_primocanes_and_floricanes.jpg/330px-PRubus_primocanes_and_floricanes.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/PRubus_primocanes_and_floricanes.jpg/440px-PRubus_primocanes_and_floricanes.jpg 2x" data-file-width="4032" data-file-height="3024" /></a><figcaption>Second-year flowering, fruiting floricanes to the left. First-year primocanes without flowers or fruit growing on the right.</figcaption></figure>
<p>Blackberries are <a href="/wiki/Perennial" title="Perennial">perennial</a> plants bearing <a href="/wiki/Biennial_plant" title="Biennial plant">biennial</a> stems (called <i>canes</i>) from their roots.<sup id="cite_ref-uga_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uga-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In its first year, a new stem, the <i>primocane</i>, reaches a full length of about 3–6 metres (9.8–19.7 feet) trailing on the ground and bearing large <a href="/wiki/Leaf_shape" class="mw-redirect" title="Leaf shape">palmate compound</a> <a href="/wiki/Leaf" title="Leaf">leaves</a> with 5–7 new leaves; it does not produce any flowers.<sup id="cite_ref-uga_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uga-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In its second year, the cane is a <i>floricane</i> with a non-growing stem.<sup id="cite_ref-uga_8-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uga-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The lateral buds open to produce flowering laterals.<sup id="cite_ref-uga_8-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uga-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> First- and second-year shoots produce short-curved, sharp <a href="/wiki/Thorns,_spines,_and_prickles" title="Thorns, spines, and prickles">thorns</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-uga_8-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uga-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Thornless cultivars have been developed during the early 21st century.<sup id="cite_ref-uga_8-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uga-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-usda18_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usda18-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Unmanaged plants tend to aggregate in a dense tangle of stems and branches,<sup id="cite_ref-king_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-king-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> which can be controlled in gardens or farms using <a href="/wiki/Trellis_(architecture)" title="Trellis (architecture)">trellises</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-carroll_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carroll-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-uga_8-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uga-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Blackberry shrubs can tolerate poor <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a>, spreading readily in wasteland, ditches, and roadsides.<sup id="cite_ref-king_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-king-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rhs_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rhs-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-blamey_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-blamey-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Flower" title="Flower">flowers</a> bloom in late spring and early summer on the tips of branches.<sup id="cite_ref-rhs_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rhs-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-uga_8-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uga-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Each flower is about <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1154941027">.mw-parser-output .frac{white-space:nowrap}.mw-parser-output .frac .num,.mw-parser-output .frac .den{font-size:80%;line-height:0;vertical-align:super}.mw-parser-output .frac .den{vertical-align:sub}.mw-parser-output .sr-only{border:0;clip:rect(0,0,0,0);clip-path:polygon(0px 0px,0px 0px,0px 0px);height:1px;margin:-1px;overflow:hidden;padding:0;position:absolute;width:1px}</style>2–3 centimetres (<span class="frac"><span class="num">3</span>&#8260;<span class="den">4</span></span>–<span class="frac">1<span class="sr-only">+</span><span class="num">1</span>&#8260;<span class="den">4</span></span> inches) in diameter, with five white-pink <a href="/wiki/Petal" title="Petal">petals</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-rhs_7-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rhs-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-uga_8-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-uga-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> The fruit only develops around ovules fertilized by the male <a href="/wiki/Gamete" title="Gamete">gamete</a> from a pollen grain.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2024)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> The most likely cause of undeveloped ovules is inadequate <a href="/wiki/Pollinator" title="Pollinator">pollinator</a> visits.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Incomplete drupelet development can signal infection with <a href="/wiki/Raspberry_bushy_dwarf_virus" class="mw-redirect" title="Raspberry bushy dwarf virus">raspberry bushy dwarf virus</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2023)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<figure typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:2020_year._Herbarium._Blackberries._img-011.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/2020_year._Herbarium._Blackberries._img-011.jpg/150px-2020_year._Herbarium._Blackberries._img-011.jpg" decoding="async" width="150" height="194" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/2020_year._Herbarium._Blackberries._img-011.jpg/225px-2020_year._Herbarium._Blackberries._img-011.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/2020_year._Herbarium._Blackberries._img-011.jpg/300px-2020_year._Herbarium._Blackberries._img-011.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2722" data-file-height="3525" /></a><figcaption>Leaf: <i>adaxial</i> side</figcaption></figure>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Genetics">Genetics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Genetics"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Locus_(genetics)" title="Locus (genetics)">loci</a> controlling the primocane fruiting was mapped in the F Locus, on LG7, whereas thorns/thornlessness was mapped on LG4.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Better understanding of the genetics is useful for genetic screening of cross-breds, and for genetic engineering purposes.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2023)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ecology">Ecology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ecology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Bee_pollinating_Blackberry.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Bee_pollinating_Blackberry.jpg/220px-Bee_pollinating_Blackberry.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="145" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Bee_pollinating_Blackberry.jpg/330px-Bee_pollinating_Blackberry.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Bee_pollinating_Blackberry.jpg/440px-Bee_pollinating_Blackberry.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1601" data-file-height="1052" /></a><figcaption>A tree bumblebee <i>(<a href="/wiki/Bombus_hypnorum" title="Bombus hypnorum">Bombus hypnorum</a>)</i> pollinating blackberries</figcaption></figure>
<p>Blackberry leaves are food for certain <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">caterpillars</a>; some grazing mammals, especially deer, are also very fond of the leaves. Caterpillars of the <a href="/wiki/Concealer_moth" class="mw-redirect" title="Concealer moth">concealer moth</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Alabonia_geoffrella" title="Alabonia geoffrella">Alabonia geoffrella</a></i> have been found feeding inside dead blackberry shoots. When mature, the berries are eaten and their seeds dispersed by mammals, such as the red fox, American black bear and the Eurasian badger, as well as by small birds.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Basket_of_wild_blackberries.JPG" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Basket_of_wild_blackberries.JPG/220px-Basket_of_wild_blackberries.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Basket_of_wild_blackberries.JPG/330px-Basket_of_wild_blackberries.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Basket_of_wild_blackberries.JPG/440px-Basket_of_wild_blackberries.JPG 2x" data-file-width="4000" data-file-height="3000" /></a><figcaption>A wild blackberry harvest</figcaption></figure>
<p>Blackberries grow wild throughout most of Europe. They are an important element in the ecology of many countries, and harvesting the berries is a common pastime. However, their vigorous growth and tendency to grow unchecked if not managed correctly means that the plants are also considered a weed, sending down <a href="/wiki/Layering" title="Layering">roots from branches that touch the ground</a>, and sending up <a href="/wiki/Sucker_(botany)" class="mw-redirect" title="Sucker (botany)">suckers</a> from the roots. In some parts of the world, such as in Australia, Chile, New Zealand, and the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a> of North America, some blackberry species, particularly <i><a href="/wiki/Rubus_armeniacus" title="Rubus armeniacus">Rubus armeniacus</a></i> (Himalayan blackberry) and <i><a href="/wiki/Rubus_laciniatus" title="Rubus laciniatus">Rubus laciniatus</a></i> (evergreen blackberry), are <a href="/wiki/Naturalisation_(biology)" title="Naturalisation (biology)">naturalized</a> and considered an <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Noxious_weed" title="Noxious weed">noxious weed</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-iscbc_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-iscbc-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-king_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-king-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-rhs_7-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-rhs-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Blackberry fruits are red when unripe, leading to an old expression that "blackberries are red when they're green".<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cultivation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Worldwide, Mexico is the leading producer of blackberries, with nearly the entire crop being produced for export into the off-season fresh markets in North America and Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-perry_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-perry-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup> Until 2018, the Mexican market was almost entirely based on the cultivar 'Tupy' (often spelled 'Tupi', but the EMBRAPA program in Brazil from which it was released prefers the 'Tupy' spelling), but Tupy fell out of favor in some Mexican growing regions.<sup id="cite_ref-tupy_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-tupy-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> In the US, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a> is the leading commercial blackberry producer, producing 19,300,000 kilograms (42,600,000 pounds) on 2,500 hectares (6,300 acres) in 2017.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-amrc_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amrc-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Numerous <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a> have been selected for commercial and amateur cultivation in Europe and the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-carroll_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carroll-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-usda18_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usda18-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Since the many species form <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybrids</a> easily, there are numerous cultivars with more than one species in their ancestry.<sup id="cite_ref-usda18_9-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usda18-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Modern <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_(biology)" title="Hybrid (biology)">hybridization</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivar</a> development took place mostly in the US. In 1880, a hybrid blackberry-raspberry named the <a href="/wiki/Loganberry" title="Loganberry">loganberry</a> was developed in <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz,_California" title="Santa Cruz, California">Santa Cruz, California</a>, by an American judge and <a href="/wiki/Horticulturalist" class="mw-redirect" title="Horticulturalist">horticulturalist</a>, <a href="/wiki/James_Harvey_Logan" title="James Harvey Logan">James Harvey Logan</a>. One of the first thornless varieties was developed in 1921, but the berries lost much of their flavor. Common thornless cultivars developed from the 1990s to the early 21st century by the <a href="/wiki/US_Department_of_Agriculture" class="mw-redirect" title="US Department of Agriculture">US Department of Agriculture</a> enabled efficient machine-harvesting, higher yields, larger and firmer fruit, and improved flavor, including the <i>Triple Crown</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-gg_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gg-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup> <i>Black Diamond</i>, <i>Black Pearl</i>, and <i>Nightfall</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Marionberry" title="Marionberry">marionberry</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-usda18_9-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usda18-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hybrids">Hybrids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hybrids"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Black_Butte_blackberry.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Black_Butte_blackberry.jpg/170px-Black_Butte_blackberry.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="259" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Black_Butte_blackberry.jpg/255px-Black_Butte_blackberry.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Black_Butte_blackberry.jpg/340px-Black_Butte_blackberry.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1831" data-file-height="2790" /></a><figcaption>Black Butte blackberry</figcaption></figure>
<p>'Marion' (marketed as "<a href="/wiki/Marionberry" title="Marionberry">marionberry</a>") is an important cultivar that was selected from seedlings from a cross between 'Chehalem' and 'Olallie' (commonly called "<a href="/wiki/Olallieberry" title="Olallieberry">Olallieberry</a>") berries.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> 'Olallie' in turn is a cross between <a href="/wiki/Loganberry" title="Loganberry">loganberry</a> and <a href="/wiki/Youngberry" title="Youngberry">youngberry</a>. 'Marion', 'Chehalem' and 'Olallie' are just three of many trailing blackberry cultivars developed by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">United States Department of Agriculture</a> <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_Research_Service" title="Agricultural Research Service">Agricultural Research Service</a> (USDA-ARS) blackberry breeding program at <a href="/wiki/Oregon_State_University" title="Oregon State University">Oregon State University</a> in <a href="/wiki/Corvallis,_Oregon" title="Corvallis, Oregon">Corvallis, Oregon</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-usda18_9-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usda18-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The most recent cultivars released from this program are the thornless cultivars 'Black Diamond', 'Black Pearl', and 'Nightfall' as well as the early-ripening 'Obsidian' and 'Metolius'. 'Black Diamond' is now the leading cultivar being planted in the Pacific Northwest. Some of the other cultivars from this program are 'Newberry', 'Waldo', 'Siskiyou', 'Black Butte', '<a href="/wiki/Kotata_Berry" class="mw-redirect" title="Kotata Berry">Kotata</a>', 'Pacific', and 'Cascade'.<sup id="cite_ref-usda18_9-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-usda18-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Varieties with good commercial characteristics developed in <a href="/wiki/Arkansas" title="Arkansas">Arkansas</a> are grown in nurseries in <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma" title="Oklahoma">Oklahoma</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-carroll_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carroll-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> Such blackberries are easy to grow, and may produce fruit for a decade or more.<sup id="cite_ref-carroll_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carroll-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> These varieties have diverse flavors varying from sweet to tart.<sup id="cite_ref-carroll_2-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-carroll-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Trailing">Trailing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Trailing"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Trailing blackberries are vigorous and crown-forming, require a <a href="/wiki/Trellis_(agriculture)" class="mw-redirect" title="Trellis (agriculture)">trellis</a> for support, and are less cold-hardy than the erect or semi-erect blackberries. In addition to the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">Pacific Northwest</a>, these types do well in similar climates, such as the United Kingdom, New Zealand, Chile, and the <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">Mediterranean countries</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2023)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Thornless">Thornless</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Thornless"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Semi-erect, prickle-free blackberries were first developed at the <a href="/wiki/John_Innes_Centre" title="John Innes Centre">John Innes Centre</a> in <a href="/wiki/Norwich" title="Norwich">Norwich</a>, UK, and subsequently by the USDA-ARS in <a href="/wiki/Beltsville,_Maryland" title="Beltsville, Maryland">Beltsville, Maryland</a>. These are crown forming and very vigorous and need a trellis for support. Cultivars include 'Black Satin', 'Chester Thornless', 'Dirksen Thornless', 'Hull Thornless', 'Loch Maree', 'Loch Ness', 'Loch Tay', 'Merton Thornless', 'Smoothstem', and 'Triple Crown'.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> 'Loch Ness' and 'Loch Tay' have gained the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Horticultural_Society" title="Royal Horticultural Society">RHS</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Award_of_Garden_Merit" title="Award of Garden Merit">Award of Garden Merit</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-RHSveg_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RHSveg-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The cultivar 'Cacanska Bestrna' (also called 'Cacak Thornless') has been developed in Serbia and has been planted on many thousands of hectares there.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Erect">Erect</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Erect"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/University_of_Arkansas" title="University of Arkansas">University of Arkansas</a> has developed cultivars of erect blackberries. These types are less vigorous than the semi-erect types and produce new canes from root initials (therefore they spread underground like raspberries). There are prickly and prickle-free cultivars from this program, including 'Navaho', 'Ouachita', 'Cherokee', 'Apache', 'Arapaho', and 'Kiowa'.<sup id="cite_ref-ge71_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ge71-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> They are also responsible for developing the primocane fruiting blackberries such as 'Prime-Jan' and 'Prime-Jim'.<sup id="cite_ref-ge71_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ge71-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Primocane">Primocane</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Primocane"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In raspberries, these types are called primocane fruiting, fall fruiting, or everbearing. 'Prime-Jim' and 'Prime-Jan' were released in 2004 by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Arkansas" title="University of Arkansas">University of Arkansas</a> and are the first cultivars of primocane fruiting blackberry.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> They grow much like the other erect cultivars described above; however, the canes that emerge in the spring will flower in midsummer and fruit in late summer or fall. The fall crop has its highest quality when it ripens in cool mild climate such as in California or the Pacific Northwest.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>'Illini Hardy', a semi-erect prickly cultivar introduced by the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois_at_Urbana%E2%80%93Champaign" class="mw-redirect" title="University of Illinois at Urbana–Champaign">University of Illinois</a>, is cane hardy in zone 5, where blackberry production has traditionally been problematic, since canes often failed to survive the winter.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2023)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mexico_and_Chile">Mexico and Chile</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Mexico and Chile"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Blackberry production in Mexico expanded considerably in the early 21st century.<sup id="cite_ref-perry_16-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-perry-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-amrc_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amrc-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> In 2017, Mexico had 97% of the <a href="/wiki/Market_share" title="Market share">market share</a> for fresh blackberries imported into the United States, while Chile had 61% of the market share for American imports of frozen blackberries.<sup id="cite_ref-amrc_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-amrc-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>While once based on the cultivar 'Brazos', an old erect blackberry cultivar developed in Texas in 1959, the Mexican industry is now dominated by the Brazilian 'Tupy' released in the 1990s. The 'Tupy' has the erect blackberry 'Comanche', and a "wild Uruguayan blackberry" as parents.<sup id="cite_ref-antunes_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-antunes-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> Since there are no native blackberries in Uruguay, the suspicion is that the widely grown '<a href="/wiki/Boysenberry" title="Boysenberry">Boysenberry</a>' is the male parent. To produce these blackberries in regions of Mexico where there is no winter chilling to stimulate flower bud development, chemical defoliation and application of growth regulators are used to bring the plants into bloom.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2023)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Diseases_and_pests">Diseases and pests</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Diseases and pests"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Blackberry_flower_(2).jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Blackberry_flower_%282%29.jpg/220px-Blackberry_flower_%282%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="176" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Blackberry_flower_%282%29.jpg/330px-Blackberry_flower_%282%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Blackberry_flower_%282%29.jpg/440px-Blackberry_flower_%282%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2336" data-file-height="1868" /></a><figcaption>The pale pink blackberry blossom</figcaption></figure>
<p>Because blackberries belong to the same genus as raspberries,<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> they share the same diseases, including <a href="/wiki/Canker" title="Canker">anthracnose</a>, which can cause the berry to have uneven ripening. Sap flow may also be slowed.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup> They also share the same remedies, including the Bordeaux mixture,<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> a combination of lime, water and <a href="/wiki/Copper(II)_sulfate" title="Copper(II) sulfate">copper(II) sulfate</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> The rows between blackberry plants must be free of weeds, blackberry suckers and grasses, which may lead to pests or diseases.<sup id="cite_ref-a-ensminger_36-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-a-ensminger-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> Fruit growers are selective when planting blackberry bushes because wild blackberries may be infected,<sup id="cite_ref-a-ensminger_36-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-a-ensminger-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> and gardeners are recommended to purchase only certified disease-free plants.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The spotted-wing drosophila, <i><a href="/wiki/Drosophila_suzukii" title="Drosophila suzukii">Drosophila suzukii</a></i>, is a serious pest of blackberries.<sup id="cite_ref-dwalsh-wsu_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dwalsh-wsu-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup> Unlike its vinegar fly relatives, which are primarily attracted to rotting or fermented fruit, <i>D. suzukii</i> attacks fresh, ripe fruit by laying eggs under the soft skin. The larvae hatch and grow in the fruit, destroying the fruit's commercial value.<sup id="cite_ref-dwalsh-wsu_38-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-dwalsh-wsu-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Another pest is <i>Amphorophora rubi</i>, known as the blackberry aphid, which eats not just blackberries but raspberries as well.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<p><i>Byturus tomentosus</i> (<a href="/wiki/Raspberry_beetle" title="Raspberry beetle">raspberry beetle</a>), <i><a href="/wiki/Lampronia_corticella" title="Lampronia corticella">Lampronia corticella</a></i> (raspberry moth) and <i><a href="/wiki/Anthonomus_rubi" title="Anthonomus rubi">Anthonomus rubi</a></i> (strawberry blossom weevil) are also known to infest blackberries.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1218072481">.mw-parser-output .infobox-subbox{padding:0;border:none;margin:-3px;width:auto;min-width:100%;font-size:100%;clear:none;float:none;background-color:transparent}.mw-parser-output .infobox-3cols-child{margin:auto}.mw-parser-output .infobox .navbar{font-size:100%}body.skin-minerva .mw-parser-output .infobox-header,body.skin-minerva .mw-parser-output .infobox-subheader,body.skin-minerva .mw-parser-output .infobox-above,body.skin-minerva .mw-parser-output .infobox-title,body.skin-minerva .mw-parser-output .infobox-image,body.skin-minerva .mw-parser-output .infobox-full-data,body.skin-minerva .mw-parser-output .infobox-below{text-align:center}html.skin-theme-clientpref-night .mw-parser-output .infobox-full-data div{background:#1f1f23!important;color:#f8f9fa}@media(prefers-color-scheme:dark){html.skin-theme-clientpref-os .mw-parser-output .infobox-full-data div{background:#1f1f23!important;color:#f8f9fa}}</style></p><table class="infobox nowrap"><caption class="infobox-title" style="white-space:normal; <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Uses"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutrients">Nutrients</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Nutrients"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Raw blackberries are 88% water, 10% <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>, 1% <a href="/wiki/Protein_(nutrient)" title="Protein (nutrient)">protein</a>, and 0.5% fat (table). In a 100 grams (3.5&#160;oz) reference amount, raw cultivated blackberries supply 43 <a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">calories</a> and rich contents (20% or more of the <a href="/wiki/Daily_Value" class="mw-redirect" title="Daily Value">Daily Value</a> (DV) of <a href="/wiki/Dietary_fiber" title="Dietary fiber">dietary fiber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">manganese</a> (31% DV), <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">vitamin C</a> (25% DV), and <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_K" title="Vitamin K">vitamin K</a> (19% DV) (table).
</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Seed_composition">Seed composition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Seed composition"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>Blackberries contain numerous large seeds that are not always preferred by consumers. The seeds contain oil rich in <a href="/wiki/Omega-3" class="mw-redirect" title="Omega-3">omega-3</a> (<a href="/wiki/Alpha-linolenic_acid" class="mw-redirect" title="Alpha-linolenic acid">alpha-linolenic acid</a>) and omega-6 (<a href="/wiki/Linoleic_acid" title="Linoleic acid">linoleic acid</a>) fats as well as <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>, dietary fiber, <a href="/wiki/Carotenoid" title="Carotenoid">carotenoids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ellagitannins" class="mw-redirect" title="Ellagitannins">ellagitannins</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ellagic_acid" title="Ellagic acid">ellagic acid</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Culinary_use">Culinary use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Culinary use"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The ripe fruit is commonly used in desserts, jams, jelly, wine and <a href="/wiki/Liqueur" title="Liqueur">liqueurs</a>. It may be mixed with other berries and fruits for pies and <a href="/wiki/Crumble" title="Crumble">crumbles</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2023)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phytochemical_research">Phytochemical research</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Phytochemical research"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Blackberries contain numerous <a href="/wiki/Phytochemicals" class="mw-redirect" title="Phytochemicals">phytochemicals</a> including <a href="/wiki/Polyphenols" class="mw-redirect" title="Polyphenols">polyphenols</a>, <a href="/wiki/Flavonoids" class="mw-redirect" title="Flavonoids">flavonoids</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anthocyanin" title="Anthocyanin">anthocyanins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Salicylic_acid" title="Salicylic acid">salicylic acid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ellagic_acid" title="Ellagic acid">ellagic acid</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Dietary_fiber" title="Dietary fiber">fiber</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Nutritiondata_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Nutritiondata-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup> Anthocyanins in blackberries are responsible for their rich dark color. One report placed blackberries at the top of more than 1,000 polyphenol-rich foods consumed in the United States,<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> but this concept of a health benefit from consuming dark-colored foods like blackberries remains scientifically unverified and not accepted for <a href="/wiki/Health_claim" title="Health claim">health claims</a> on food labels.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_uses">Historical uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Historical uses"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One of the earliest known instances of blackberry consumption comes from the remains of the <a href="/wiki/Haraldsk%C3%A6r_Woman" title="Haraldskær Woman">Haraldskær Woman</a>, the naturally preserved <a href="/wiki/Bog_body" title="Bog body">bog body</a> of a Danish woman dating from approximately 2,500 years ago. Forensic evidence found blackberries in her stomach contents, among other foods. The use of blackberries to make wines and <a href="/wiki/Cordial_(medicine)" title="Cordial (medicine)">cordials</a> was documented in the <a href="/wiki/London_Pharmacopoeia" class="mw-redirect" title="London Pharmacopoeia">London Pharmacopoeia</a> in 1696.<sup id="cite_ref-gg_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gg-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> In the culinary world, blackberries have a long history of use alongside other fruits to make pies, jellies and jams.<sup id="cite_ref-gg_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gg-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Blackberry plants were used for <a href="/wiki/Traditional_medicine" title="Traditional medicine">traditional medicine</a> by Greeks, other European peoples, and aboriginal Americans.<sup id="cite_ref-gg_21-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gg-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> A 1771 document described brewing blackberry leaves, stem, and bark for <a href="/wiki/Peptic_ulcer_disease" title="Peptic ulcer disease">stomach ulcers</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-gg_21-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gg-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Blackberry fruit, leaves, and stems have been used to dye fabrics and hair. Native Americans have even been known to use the stems to make rope. The shrubs have also been used for barriers around buildings, crops and livestock. The wild plants have sharp, thick prickles, which offered some protection against enemies and large animals.<sup id="cite_ref-gg_21-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gg-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">In culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Blackberry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: In culture"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Folklore in the United Kingdom and Ireland tells that blackberries should not be picked after <a href="/wiki/Old_Michaelmas_Day" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Michaelmas Day">Old Michaelmas Day</a> (11 October) as the <a href="/wiki/Devil" title="Devil">devil</a> (or a <a href="/wiki/P%C3%BAca" title="Púca">Púca</a>) has made them unfit to eat by stepping, spitting or fouling on them.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> There is some value in this legend as autumn's wetter and cooler weather often allows the fruit to become infected by various <a href="/wiki/Mold_(fungus)" class="mw-redirect" title="Mold (fungus)">molds</a> such as <i><a href="/wiki/Botryotinia" title="Botryotinia">Botryotinia</a></i> which give the fruit an unpleasant look and may be toxic.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> According to some traditions, a blackberry's deep purple color represents Jesus' blood and the <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_thorns" title="Crown of thorns">crown of thorns</a> was made of brambles,<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> although other thorny plants, such as <i><a href="/wiki/Crataegus" title="Crataegus">Crataegus</a></i> (hawthorn) and <i><a href="/wiki/Euphorbia_milii" title="Euphorbia milii">Euphorbia milii</a></i> (crown of thorns plant), have been proposed as the material for the crown.<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
