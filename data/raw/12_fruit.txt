<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuzu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>This fruit resembles a yellow <a href="/wiki/Clementine" title="Clementine">clementine</a> with uneven skin and can be either yellow or green depending on the degree of ripeness. <i>Yuzu</i> fruits, which are very aromatic, typically range between <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1154941027">.mw-parser-output .frac{white-space:nowrap}.mw-parser-output .frac .num,.mw-parser-output .frac .den{font-size:80%;line-height:0;vertical-align:super}.mw-parser-output .frac .den{vertical-align:sub}.mw-parser-output .sr-only{border:0;clip:rect(0,0,0,0);clip-path:polygon(0px 0px,0px 0px,0px 0px);height:1px;margin:-1px;overflow:hidden;padding:0;position:absolute;width:1px}</style>5.5 and 7.5&#160;cm (<span class="frac">2<span class="sr-only">+</span><span class="num">1</span>&#8260;<span class="den">8</span></span> and 3&#160;in) in diameter but can be as large as a regular grapefruit (up to 10&#160;cm or 4&#160;in, or larger).
</p><p>Yuzu forms an upright shrub or small tree, which commonly has many large thorns. Leaves are notable for a large, leaf-like <a href="/wiki/Petiole_(botany)" title="Petiole (botany)">petiole</a>, resembling those of the related <a href="/wiki/Makrut_lime" class="mw-redirect" title="Makrut lime">makrut lime</a> and ichang <a href="/wiki/Papeda_(citrus)" title="Papeda (citrus)">papeda</a>, and are heavily scented.
</p><p>Yuzu closely resembles <a href="/wiki/Sudachi" title="Sudachi">sudachi</a> (<i>Citrus sudachi</i>, a <a href="/wiki/Japanese_citrus" title="Japanese citrus">Japanese citrus</a> from Tokushima Prefecture, a yuzu–mandarin orange cross) in many regards, though, unlike the sudachi, yuzu eventually ripen to an orange colour and there are subtle differences between the flavors of the fruit.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuzu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cultivation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The yuzu originated and grew wild in central China and Tibet region. It was introduced to Japan and Korea during the <a href="/wiki/Tang_dynasty" title="Tang dynasty">Tang dynasty</a> and is still cultivated there.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> It grows slowly, generally requiring ten years to fruit.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> To shorten the duration to fruiting, it may be grafted onto karatachi (<a href="/wiki/Trifoliate_orange" title="Trifoliate orange"><i>P. trifoliata</i></a>).<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> It is unusual among citrus plants in being relatively frost-hardy, due to its cold-hardy <a href="/wiki/Citrus_cavaleriei" title="Citrus cavaleriei">Ichang papeda</a> ancestry, and can be grown in regions with winters as low as −7&#160;°C (19&#160;°F) where more sensitive citrus would not thrive.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Varieties_and_similar_fruits">Varieties and similar fruits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuzu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Varieties and similar fruits"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, an ornamental version of yuzu called <i>hana yuzu</i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">花柚子, 花ゆず</span></span>)</span> "flower yuzu" is also grown for its flowers rather than its fruit. A sweet variety of yuzu known as the <i>yuko</i>, only present in Japan, became severely endangered during the 1970s and 1980s; a major attempt has been made to revive this varietal in southern Japan.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Another variety of yuzu in Japan, with knobby skin, is called <i>shishi yuzu</i><span style="font-weight: normal"> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">獅子柚子</span></span>, literally "lion yuzu")</span>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p><a href="/wiki/Dangyuja" title="Dangyuja">Dangyuja</a>, a Korean citrus fruit from <a href="/wiki/Jeju_Island" title="Jeju Island">Jeju Island</a>, is often considered a type of yuzu due to its similar shape and flavor, but it is genetically a variety of <a href="/wiki/Pomelo" title="Pomelo">pomelo</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use">Use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuzu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Use"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="East_Asia">East Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuzu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: East Asia"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Culinary_use">Culinary use</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuzu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Culinary use"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuzu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Japan"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Yuzuvinegar.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Yuzuvinegar.jpg/220px-Yuzuvinegar.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="336" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Yuzuvinegar.jpg/330px-Yuzuvinegar.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Yuzuvinegar.jpg/440px-Yuzuvinegar.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1326" data-file-height="2025" /></a><figcaption>A bottle of yuzu vinegar</figcaption></figure>
<p>Yuzu's domestic production is about 27,000 <a href="/wiki/Ton" title="Ton">tons</a> (2016).<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> Though rarely eaten as a fruit, yuzu is a common ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Japanese_cuisine" title="Japanese cuisine">Japanese cuisine</a>, where the aromatic <a href="/wiki/Zest_(ingredient)" title="Zest (ingredient)">zest</a> (outer rind) and the juice are used much in the same way that <a href="/wiki/Lemon" title="Lemon">lemons</a> are used in other cuisines.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> The yuzu's flavor is tart and fragrant, closely resembling that of the grapefruit, with overtones of <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_orange" title="Mandarin orange">mandarin orange</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-:0_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is an integral ingredient (along with <a href="/wiki/Sudachi" title="Sudachi">sudachi</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daidai" title="Daidai">daidai</a>, and other similar citrus fruits) in the citrus-based sauce <a href="/wiki/Ponzu" title="Ponzu">ponzu</a>, and yuzu <a href="/wiki/Vinegar" title="Vinegar">vinegar</a> is also produced.<sup id="cite_ref-:3_9-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:3-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup> Yuzu is often combined with honey to make <span title="Japanese-language text"><i lang="ja-Latn">yuzu hachimitsu</i></span> (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">柚子蜂蜜</span></span>), a kind of syrup that is used to make yuzu tea (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">柚子茶</span></span>), or as an ingredient in alcoholic drinks such as the yuzu sour (<span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">柚子サワー</span></span>).<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <span title="Japanese-language text"><i lang="ja-Latn"><a href="/wiki/Yuzukosh%C5%8D" class="mw-redirect" title="Yuzukoshō">Yuzu kosho</a></i></span> (also <span title="Japanese-language text"><i lang="ja-Latn">yuzukosho</i></span>, literally "yuzu and pepper") is a spicy Japanese sauce made from green or yellow yuzu zest, green or red <a href="/wiki/Chili_pepper" title="Chili pepper">chili peppers</a>, and salt.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It is used to make liquor (such as <span title="Japanese-language text"><i lang="ja-Latn">yuzukomachi</i></span>, <span title="Japanese-language text"><span lang="ja">柚子小町</span></span>) and wine.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Slivered yuzu rind is used to garnish a savory, salty egg-pudding dish called <i><a href="/wiki/Chawanmushi" title="Chawanmushi">chawanmushi</a></i>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Miso_soup" title="Miso soup">miso soup</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> It is often used along with <span title="Japanese-language text"><i lang="ja-Latn"><a href="/wiki/Sudachi" title="Sudachi">sudachi</a></i></span> and <span title="Japanese-language text"><i lang="ja-Latn"><a href="/wiki/Kabosu" title="Kabosu">kabosu</a></i></span>. Yuzu is used to make various sweets, including <a href="/wiki/Marmalade" title="Marmalade">marmalade</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cake" title="Cake">cake</a>. It is used extensively in the flavoring of many snack products, such as <a href="/wiki/Doritos" title="Doritos">Doritos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-soranews_20150922_jagariko_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-soranews_20150922_jagariko-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-digjapan_20170111_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-digjapan_20170111-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NHK_WOrld_20171218_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NHK_WOrld_20171218-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Korea">Korea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuzu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Korea"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Yuja-cha" class="mw-redirect" title="Yuja-cha">Yuja-cha</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yuja-hwachae" title="Yuja-hwachae">Yuja-hwachae</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Korean_cuisine" title="Korean cuisine">Korean cuisine</a>, yuja is most commonly used to make <span title="Korean-language text"><i lang="ko-Latn"><a href="/wiki/Yuja-cheong" class="mw-redirect" title="Yuja-cheong">yuja-cheong</a></i></span> (<span title="Korean-language text"><span lang="ko">유자청</span></span>, yuja marmalade) and <a href="/wiki/Yuja_tea" title="Yuja tea">yuja tea</a>. <span title="Korean-language text"><i lang="ko-Latn">Yuja-cheong</i></span> can be made by <a href="/wiki/Sugaring" title="Sugaring">sugaring</a> peeled, depulped, and thinly sliced yuja, and <span title="Korean-language text"><i lang="ko-Latn">yuja-cha</i></span>, yuja tea, can be made by mixing hot water with <span title="Korean-language text"><i lang="ko-Latn">yuja-cheong</i></span>.<sup id="cite_ref-:1_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:1-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> <span title="Korean-language text"><i lang="ko-Latn"><a href="/wiki/Yuja-hwachae" title="Yuja-hwachae">Yuja-hwachae</a></i></span> (<span title="Korean-language text"><span lang="ko">유자화채</span></span>, yuja punch), a variety of <span title="Korean-language text"><i lang="ko-Latn"><a href="/wiki/Hwachae" title="Hwachae">hwachae</a></i></span> (fruit punch), is another common dessert made with yuja. Yuja is also a common ingredient in <a href="/wiki/Fusion_cuisine" title="Fusion cuisine">Korean-style western food</a>, such as salads.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">Other uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuzu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other uses"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Yuzu_baths">Yuzu baths</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuzu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Yuzu baths"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Yuzu_bath" title="Yuzu bath">Yuzu bath</a></div>
<p>Yuzu is also known for its characteristically strong aroma, and the oil from its skin is marketed as a fragrance. In Japan, bathing with yuzu on <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">Tōji</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">winter solstice</a>, is a custom that dates to at least the early 18th century.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-kokugo-yuzuyu_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kokugo-yuzuyu-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> Whole yuzu fruits are floated in the hot water of the bath, sometimes enclosed in a cloth bag, releasing their aroma.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> The fruit may also be cut in half, allowing the citrus juice to mingle with the bathwater. The yuzu bath, known commonly as <i>yuzu yu</i> (柚子湯), but also as <i>yuzu buro</i> (柚子風呂), is said to guard against colds, treat the roughness of skin, warm the body, and relax the mind.<sup id="cite_ref-kokugo-yuzuyu_20-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kokugo-yuzuyu-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h5><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_as_wood">Use as wood</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuzu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Use as wood"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h5>
<p>The body of the <i><a href="/wiki/Taepyeongso" title="Taepyeongso">taepyeongso</a></i>, a Korean traditional oboe, close to the Chinese <i><a href="/wiki/Suona" title="Suona">suona</a></i> or the <a href="/wiki/Zurna" title="Zurna">zurna</a>, is often made from <a href="/wiki/Jujube" title="Jujube">jujube</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mulberry" class="mw-redirect" title="Mulberry">mulberry</a>, or yuzu wood.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Elsewhere">Elsewhere</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Yuzu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Elsewhere"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Beginning in the early 21st century, yuzu has been increasingly used by chefs in the United States and other Western nations, achieving notice in a 2003 article in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-karp2003_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-karp2003-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Agriculture" title="United States Department of Agriculture">Department of Agriculture</a> has a ban on the import of fresh yuzu (alongside most citrus plants) from abroad, including both the fruit and the trees.<sup id="cite_ref-new_yorker_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-new_yorker-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> This is intended to prevent the spread of contagious diseases amongst domestic crops.<sup id="cite_ref-karp2003_23-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-karp2003-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-sweetgrass2017_25-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-sweetgrass2017-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> However, following its introduction to California in 1888, yuzu is cultivated and available for sale in the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-karp2003_23-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-karp2003-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
