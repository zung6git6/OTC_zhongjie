<p><b>Kumquats</b> (<span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="/ʌ/: &#39;u&#39; in &#39;cut&#39;">ʌ</span><span title="&#39;m&#39; in &#39;my&#39;">m</span><span title="&#39;k&#39; in &#39;kind&#39;">k</span><span title="&#39;w&#39; in &#39;wind&#39;">w</span><span title="/ɒ/: &#39;o&#39; in &#39;body&#39;">ɒ</span><span title="&#39;t&#39; in &#39;tie&#39;">t</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span style="font-size:90%">KUM</span>-kwot</i></a>),<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> or <b>cumquats</b> in <a href="/wiki/Australian_English" title="Australian English">Australian English</a>, are a group of small, <a href="/wiki/Angiosperm" class="mw-redirect" title="Angiosperm">angiosperm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a>-bearing <a href="/wiki/Tree" title="Tree">trees</a> in the family <a href="/wiki/Rutaceae" title="Rutaceae">Rutaceae</a>. Their <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy" title="Taxonomy">taxonomy</a> is disputed. They were previously classified as forming the now-historical genus <i>Fortunella</i> or placed within <i><a href="/wiki/Citrus" title="Citrus">Citrus</a></i>, <span title="Latin-language text"><i lang="la"><a href="/wiki/Sensu_lato" class="mw-redirect" title="Sensu lato">sensu lato</a></i></span>. Different classifications have alternatively assigned them to anywhere from a single species, <i><a href="/wiki/Citrus_japonica" title="Citrus japonica">Citrus japonica</a></i>, to numerous species representing each cultivar. Recent genomic analysis defines three pure species, <i>Citrus hindsii</i>, <i>C. margarita</i> and <i>C. crassifolia</i>, with <i>C.</i> × <i>japonica</i> being a hybrid of the last two.
</p><p>The edible fruit closely resembles the <a href="/wiki/Orange_(fruit)" title="Orange (fruit)">orange</a> (<i>Citrus sinensis</i>) in color, texture, and anatomy, but is much smaller, being approximately the size of a large <a href="/wiki/Olive" title="Olive">olive</a>. The kumquat is a fairly <a href="/wiki/Cold-hardy_citrus" title="Cold-hardy citrus">cold-hardy citrus</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumquat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The English word <i>kumquat</i> is a borrowing of the <a href="/wiki/Cantonese" title="Cantonese">Cantonese</a> <span title="Yue Chinese-language romanization"><i lang="yue-Latn">gām gwāt</i></span> (<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1177148991">.mw-parser-output .IPA-label-small{font-size:85%}.mw-parser-output .references .IPA-label-small,.mw-parser-output .infobox .IPA-label-small,.mw-parser-output .navbox .IPA-label-small{font-size:100%}</style><span class="IPA-label IPA-label-small">IPA:</span> <span class="IPA nowrap" lang="yue-Latn-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Cantonese" title="Help:IPA/Cantonese">&#91;kɐ́m<span class="wrap"> </span>kʷɐ́t̚&#93;</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh">金橘</span>), from <span title="Yue Chinese-language romanization"><i lang="yue-Latn">gām</i></span> "golden" + <span title="Yue Chinese-language romanization"><i lang="yue-Latn">gwāt</i></span> "orange".<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumquat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kumquat plants have thornless branches and extremely glossy <a href="/wiki/Leaves" class="mw-redirect" title="Leaves">leaves</a>. They bear dainty white flowers that occur in clusters or individually inside the leaf axils. The plants can reach a height from 2.5 to 4.5 metres (8 to 15 feet), with dense branches, sometimes bearing small thorns.<sup id="cite_ref-fgcu_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fgcu-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> They bear yellowish-orange fruits that are oval or round in shape. The fruits can be 2.5–5 centimetres (1–2 inches) in diameter and have a sweet, pulpy skin and slightly acidic inner pulp. The fruit is often eaten whole by humans and has a taste which is sweet and somewhat sour.<sup id="cite_ref-Experts_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Experts-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Kumquat trees are <a href="/wiki/Self-pollination" title="Self-pollination">self-pollinating</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Species">Species</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumquat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Species"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Citrus_taxonomy" title="Citrus taxonomy">Citrus taxonomy</a> is complicated and controversial. Different systems place various types of kumquats in different species or unite them into as few as two species. Botanically, many of the varieties of kumquats are classified as their own species, rather than a cultivar.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2023)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup> Historically they were viewed as falling within the genus <i>Citrus</i>, but the <a href="/wiki/Citrus_taxonomy#Citrus_naming_systems" title="Citrus taxonomy">Swingle system</a> of citrus taxonomy elevated them to their own genus, <i>Fortunella</i>. Recent phylogenetic analysis suggests they do fall within <i>Citrus</i>. Swingle divided the kumquats into two subgenera, the <i>Protocitrus</i>, containing the primitive Hong Kong kumquat, and <i>Eufortunella</i>, comprising the round, oval kumquat, Meiwa kumquats,<sup id="cite_ref-Swingle_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Swingle-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> to which Tanaka added two others, the Malayan kumquat and the Jiangsu kumquat. Chromosomal analysis suggested that Swingle's <i>Eufortunella</i> represent a single 'true' species, while Tanaka's additional species were revealed to be likely hybrids of <i>Fortunella</i> with other <i>Citrus</i>, so-called x<i><a href="/wiki/Citrofortunella" title="Citrofortunella">Citrofortunella</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-Yasada_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Yasada-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>One recent genomic analysis concluded there was only one true species of kumquat, but the analysis did not include the Hong Kong variety seen as a distinct species in all earlier analyses.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> A 2020 review concluded that genomic data were insufficient to reach a definitive conclusion on which kumquat cultivars represented distinct species.<sup id="cite_ref-citrus_taxonomy_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-citrus_taxonomy-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> In 2022, a genome-level analysis of cultivated and wild varieties drew several conclusions. The authors found support for the division of kumquats into subgenera: <i>Protocitrus</i>, for the wild Hong Kong variety, and <i>Eufortunella</i> for the cultivated varieties, with a divergence predating the end of the <a href="/wiki/Quaternary_glaciation" title="Quaternary glaciation">Quaternary glaciation</a>, perhaps between two ancestral populations isolated south and north, respectively, of the <a href="/wiki/Nanling_Mountains" title="Nanling Mountains">Nanling mountain range</a>. Within the latter group, the oval, round and Meiwa kumquat each showed a level of divergence greater than between other recognized citrus species, such as between pomelo and citron, and hence each merits species-level classification. Though Swingle had speculated that the Meiwa kumquat was a hybrid of oval and round kumquats, the genomic analysis suggested instead that the round kumquat was an oval/Meiwa hybrid.<sup id="cite_ref-FASE_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FASE-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_habitat">Distribution and habitat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumquat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution and habitat"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The kumquat plant is native to Southern <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Purdue_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> The historical reference to kumquats appears in literature of China from at least the 12th century.<sup id="cite_ref-fgcu_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fgcu-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> They have been cultivated for centuries in other parts of East Asia, South Asia, and Southeast Asia.<sup id="cite_ref-Experts_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Experts-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> They were introduced into <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> in 1846 by <a href="/wiki/Robert_Fortune" title="Robert Fortune">Robert Fortune</a>, collector for the <a href="/wiki/London_Horticultural_Society" class="mw-redirect" title="London Horticultural Society">London Horticultural Society</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> and are now found across the world.<sup id="cite_ref-Experts_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Experts-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumquat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cultivation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:A_lemon_plant_(Citrus_japonica);_flowering_and_fruiting_stem_Wellcome_V0044760.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img alt="Illustration, Walter Hood Fitch" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/A_lemon_plant_%28Citrus_japonica%29%3B_flowering_and_fruiting_stem_Wellcome_V0044760.jpg/170px-A_lemon_plant_%28Citrus_japonica%29%3B_flowering_and_fruiting_stem_Wellcome_V0044760.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="250" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/A_lemon_plant_%28Citrus_japonica%29%3B_flowering_and_fruiting_stem_Wellcome_V0044760.jpg/255px-A_lemon_plant_%28Citrus_japonica%29%3B_flowering_and_fruiting_stem_Wellcome_V0044760.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/A_lemon_plant_%28Citrus_japonica%29%3B_flowering_and_fruiting_stem_Wellcome_V0044760.jpg/340px-A_lemon_plant_%28Citrus_japonica%29%3B_flowering_and_fruiting_stem_Wellcome_V0044760.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2188" data-file-height="3222" /></a><figcaption>Illustration by <a href="/wiki/Walter_Hood_Fitch" title="Walter Hood Fitch">Walter Hood Fitch</a></figcaption></figure>
<p>Kumquats are much <a href="/wiki/Hardiness_(plants)" title="Hardiness (plants)">hardier</a> than citrus plants such as <a href="/wiki/Orange_(fruit)" title="Orange (fruit)">oranges</a>. Sowing seed in the spring is most ideal because the temperature is pleasant with more chances of rain and sunshine. This also gives the tree enough time to become well established before winter. Early spring is the best time to transplant a sapling. They do best in direct sunlight (needing 6–7 hours a day) and planted directly in the ground. Kumquats do well in USDA hardy zones 9 and 10 and can survive in temperatures as low as 18 degrees F (-7 degrees C). On trees mature enough, kumquats will form in about 90 days.
</p><p>In cultivation in the <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">UK</a>, <i>Citrus japonica</i> has gained the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Horticultural_Society" title="Royal Horticultural Society">Royal Horticultural Society</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Award_of_Garden_Merit" title="Award of Garden Merit">Award of Garden Merit</a><sup id="cite_ref-RHSPF_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RHSPF-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> (confirmed 2017).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Propagation">Propagation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumquat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Propagation"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Kumquats do not grow well from seeds and so are <a href="/wiki/Vegetative_reproduction" title="Vegetative reproduction">vegetatively propagated</a> by using <a href="/wiki/Citrus_rootstock" title="Citrus rootstock">rootstock</a> of another citrus fruit,<sup id="cite_ref-Purdue_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Air_layering" class="mw-redirect" title="Air layering">air layering</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Cutting_(plant)" title="Cutting (plant)">cuttings</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Purdue_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Varieties">Varieties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumquat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Varieties"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <i>Nordmann seedless</i> is a seedless cultivar of the Nagami kumquat (<i><a href="/wiki/Citrus_margarita" title="Citrus margarita">Citrus margarita</a></i>). It is similar to Nagami but with a slightly different shape and lighter skin.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The C<i>entennial Variegated</i> is another cultivar of the Nagami kumquat. It originated from the open pollination of a Nagami kumquat tree. The fruits are striped light green and yellow when underripe, and turn orange and lose their stripes when they ripen. They are oval-shaped, necked, 2.5 inches long and have a smooth rind. They mature in winter.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup> This <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivar</a> arose <a href="/wiki/Mutation" title="Mutation">spontaneously</a> from the oval kumquat (<i><a href="/wiki/Citrus_margarita" title="Citrus margarita">Citrus margarita</a></i>). It produces a greater proportion of fruit to peel than the oval kumquat, and the fruit are rounder and sometimes necked. Fruit are distinguishable by their variegation in color, exhibiting bright green and yellow stripes,<sup id="cite_ref-Purdue_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and by its lack of thorns.
</p><p>The <i>Puchimaru kumquat</i> is a seedless or virtually seedless Japanese kumquat cultivar. It is resistant to citrus canker and citrus scab. The fruit weighs 11–20 grams and is ellipsoid in shape. It has a dark orange rind which is 4 millimeters thick. The juice content is relatively low. The oil glands are somewhat large and conspicuous. It ripens in January.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Essential_oil">Essential oil</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Kumquat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Essential oil"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Essential_oil" title="Essential oil">essential oil</a> of the kumquat peel contains much of the aroma of the fruit, and is composed principally of <a href="/wiki/Limonene" title="Limonene">limonene</a>, which makes up around 93% of the total.<sup id="cite_ref-KoyasakoBernhard_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KoyasakoBernhard-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup> Besides limonene and <a href="/wiki/Alpha-pinene" class="mw-redirect" title="Alpha-pinene">alpha-pinene</a> (0.34%), both of which are considered <a href="/wiki/Monoterpene" title="Monoterpene">monoterpenes</a>, the oil is unusually rich (0.38% total) in <a href="/wiki/Sesquiterpene" title="Sesquiterpene">sesquiterpenes</a> such as α-<a href="/wiki/Bergamotene" title="Bergamotene">bergamotene</a> (0.021%), <a href="/wiki/Caryophyllene" title="Caryophyllene">caryophyllene</a> (0.18%), α-<a href="/wiki/Humulene" title="Humulene">humulene</a> (0.07%) and α-muurolene (0.06%), and these contribute to the spicy and woody flavor of the fruit. <a href="/wiki/Carbonyl" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbonyl">Carbonyl</a> compounds make up much of the remainder, and these are responsible for much of the distinctive flavor. These compounds include <a href="/wiki/Ester" title="Ester">esters</a> such as isopropyl propanoate (1.8%) and terpinyl acetate (1.26%); <a href="/wiki/Ketone" title="Ketone">ketones</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Carvone" title="Carvone">carvone</a> (0.175%); and a range of <a href="/wiki/Aldehyde" title="Aldehyde">aldehydes</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Citronellal" title="Citronellal">citronellal</a> (0.6%) and <a href="/wiki/2-Methylundecanal" title="2-Methylundecanal">2-methylundecanal</a>. Other oxygenated compounds include <a href="/wiki/Nerol" title="Nerol">nerol</a> (0.22%) and Trans-lialool oxide (0.15%).<sup id="cite_ref-KoyasakoBernhard_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-KoyasakoBernhard-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
