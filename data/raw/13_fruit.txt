in &#39;lie&#39;">l</span><span title="/iː/: &#39;ee&#39; in &#39;fleece&#39;">iː</span><span title="/tʃ/: &#39;ch&#39; in &#39;China&#39;">tʃ</span><span title="/iː/: &#39;ee&#39; in &#39;fleece&#39;">iː</span></span>/</a></span></span> <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key"><i title="English pronunciation respelling"><span style="font-size:90%">LEE</span>-chee</i></a>, <span class="rt-commentedText nowrap"><link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1177148991"><span class="IPA-label IPA-label-small"><a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">UK</a>: </span><span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt" lang="en-fonipa"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="&#39;l&#39; <i><b>Litchi chinensis</b></i>; <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">Chinese</a>&#58; <span lang="zh"><a href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E8%8D%94%E6%9E%9D" class="extiw" title="wikt:荔枝">荔枝</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">pinyin</a>&#58; <i><span lang="zh-Latn">lìzhī</span></i>; <a href="/wiki/Jyutping" title="Jyutping">Jyutping</a>&#58; <i><span lang="yue-Latn-jyutping">lai6 zi1</span></i>; </p><p>It is a <a href="/wiki/Tropical" class="mw-redirect" title="Tropical">tropical</a> tree native to <a href="/wiki/South_China" title="South China">South China</a>, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, and northern <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Purdue_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-kew_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kew-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> The tree has been introduced throughout Southeast Asia and <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-kew_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kew-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup> Cultivation in China is documented from the 11th century.<sup id="cite_ref-Purdue_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> China is the main producer of lychees, followed by <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, India, other countries in Southeast Asia, other countries in the <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">South Asia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">Madagascar</a>, and <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a>. A tall <a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">evergreen</a> tree, it bears small fleshy sweet <a href="/wiki/Drupe" title="Drupe">fruits</a>. The outside of the fruit is a pink-red, rough-textured soft shell.
</p><p>Lychee seeds contain <a href="/wiki/Methylene_cyclopropyl_acetic_acid" title="Methylene cyclopropyl acetic acid">methylene cyclopropyl glycine</a> which has caused <a href="/wiki/Hypoglycemia" title="Hypoglycemia">hypoglycemia</a> associated with outbreaks of <a href="/wiki/Encephalopathy" title="Encephalopathy">encephalopathy</a> in undernourished Indian and Vietnamese children who consumed lychee fruit.<sup id="cite_ref-lancet_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lancet-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lancet2_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lancet2-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Taxonomy">Taxonomy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lychee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Taxonomy"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Sonnerat_litchi.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Sonnerat_litchi.jpg/170px-Sonnerat_litchi.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="225" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Sonnerat_litchi.jpg/255px-Sonnerat_litchi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Sonnerat_litchi.jpg/340px-Sonnerat_litchi.jpg 2x" data-file-width="439" data-file-height="582" /></a><figcaption><a href="/wiki/Pierre_Sonnerat" title="Pierre Sonnerat">Pierre Sonnerat</a>'s drawing from <i>Voyage aux Indes Orientales et à la Chine</i> (1782)<sup id="cite_ref-Sonnerat_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sonnerat-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></figcaption></figure>
<p><i>Litchi chinensis</i>  is the <a href="/wiki/Monotypic_taxon" title="Monotypic taxon">sole member</a> of the genus <i>Litchi</i> in the <a href="/wiki/Sapindus" title="Sapindus">soapberry</a> family, <a href="/wiki/Sapindaceae" title="Sapindaceae">Sapindaceae</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Purdue_4-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>It was described and named by French naturalist <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Sonnerat" title="Pierre Sonnerat">Pierre Sonnerat</a> in his account "<i>Voyage aux Indes Orientales et à la Chine, fait depuis 1774 jusqu'à 1781</i>" (translation: "Voyage to the East Indies and China, made between 1774 and 1781"), which was published in 1782.<sup id="cite_ref-Sonnerat_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sonnerat-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> There are three subspecies, determined by flower arrangement, twig thickness, fruit, and a number of <a href="/wiki/Stamen" title="Stamen">stamens</a>.
</p>
<ul><li><i>Litchi chinensis</i> subsp. <i>chinensis</i> is the only commercialized lychee. It grows wild in <a href="/wiki/South_China" title="South China">southern China</a>, northern <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">Cambodia</a>. It has thin twigs, flowers typically have six stamens, fruit are smooth or with protuberances up to 2&#160;mm (0.079&#160;in).</li>
<li><i>Litchi chinensis</i> subsp. <i>philippinensis</i> (Radlk.) Leenh. It is common in the wild in the <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">Philippines</a> and rarely cultivated. It has thin twigs, six to seven stamens, long oval fruit with spiky protuberances up to 3&#160;mm (0.12&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-USDA2_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-USDA2-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup></li>
<li><i>Litchi chinensis</i> subsp. <i>javensis</i>. It is only known in cultivation, in <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a> and <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">Indonesia</a>. It has thick twigs, flowers with seven to eleven stamens in sessile clusters, smooth fruit with protuberances up to 1&#160;mm (0.039&#160;in).<sup id="cite_ref-Purdue_4-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Menzel_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Menzel-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lychee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tree">Tree</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lychee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tree"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Litchi chinensis</i> is an evergreen tree that is frequently less than 15&#160;m (49&#160;ft) tall, sometimes reaching 28&#160;m (92&#160;ft).<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Its evergreen leaves, 12.5 to 20&#160;cm (4.9 to 7.9&#160;in) long, are pinnate, having 4 to 8 alternate, elliptic-oblong to lanceolate, abruptly pointed, leaflets,
</p><p>The bark is grey-black, the branches a brownish-red. Its <a href="/wiki/Evergreen" title="Evergreen">evergreen</a> leaves are 12.5 to 20&#160;cm (4.9 to 7.9&#160;in) long, with leaflets in two to four pairs.<sup id="cite_ref-Purdue_4-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Lychee are similar in foliage to the family <a href="/wiki/Lauraceae" title="Lauraceae">Lauraceae</a>, likely due to <a href="/wiki/Convergent_evolution" title="Convergent evolution">convergent evolution</a>. They are adapted by developing leaves that repel water, and are called laurophyll or <a href="/wiki/Lauroid" class="mw-redirect" title="Lauroid">lauroid</a> leaves.
</p><p>Flowers grow on a terminal <a href="/wiki/Inflorescence" title="Inflorescence">inflorescence</a> with many <a href="/wiki/Panicle" title="Panicle">panicles</a> on the current season's growth. The panicles grow in clusters of ten or more, reaching 10 to 40&#160;cm (3.9 to 15.7&#160;in) or longer, holding hundreds of small white, yellow, or green flowers that are distinctively fragrant.<sup id="cite_ref-Menzel_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Menzel-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fruit">Fruit</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lychee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fruit"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Lychee_fruits_and_seed.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Lychee_fruits_and_seed.jpg/220px-Lychee_fruits_and_seed.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Lychee_fruits_and_seed.jpg/330px-Lychee_fruits_and_seed.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Lychee_fruits_and_seed.jpg/440px-Lychee_fruits_and_seed.jpg 2x" data-file-width="6000" data-file-height="4000" /></a><figcaption>Whole and opened fruit with seed</figcaption></figure>
<p>The lychee bears fleshy <a href="/wiki/Drupe" title="Drupe">fruits</a> that mature in 80–112 days depending on climate, location, and cultivar. Fruits vary in shape from round to ovoid to heart-shaped, up to 5&#160;cm long and 4&#160;cm wide (2.0&#160;in&#160;× 1.6&#160;in), weighing approximately 20 g.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Barret_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barret-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The thin, tough skin is green when immature, ripening to red or pink-red, and is smooth or covered with small sharp protuberances roughly textured. The <a href="/wiki/Peel_(fruit)" title="Peel (fruit)">rind</a> is inedible but easily removed to expose a layer of translucent white fleshy <a href="/wiki/Aril" title="Aril">aril</a> with a floral smell and a sweet flavor.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> The skin turns brown and dry when left out after harvesting.
</p><p>The fleshy, edible portion of the fruit is an <a href="/wiki/Aril" title="Aril">aril</a>, surrounding one dark brown inedible seed that is 1 to 3.3&#160;cm long and 0.6 to 1.2&#160;cm wide (0.39–1.30 by 0.24–0.47&#160;in). Some cultivars produce a high percentage of fruits with shriveled aborted seeds known as 'chicken tongues'. These fruits typically have a higher price, due to having more edible flesh.<sup id="cite_ref-Menzel_10-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Menzel-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Since the floral flavor is lost in the process of canning, the fruit is usually eaten fresh.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lychee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Flora_Sinensis_-_Lychee.JPG" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Flora_Sinensis_-_Lychee.JPG/220px-Flora_Sinensis_-_Lychee.JPG" decoding="async" width="220" height="299" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Flora_Sinensis_-_Lychee.JPG/330px-Flora_Sinensis_-_Lychee.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Flora_Sinensis_-_Lychee.JPG/440px-Flora_Sinensis_-_Lychee.JPG 2x" data-file-width="617" data-file-height="839" /></a><figcaption>"Lici Fruit Tree" in <a href="/wiki/Michal_Boym" class="mw-redirect" title="Michal Boym">Michal Boym</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Flora_Sinensis" title="Flora Sinensis">Flora Sinensis</a></i> (1657)</figcaption></figure>
<p>Cultivation of lychee began in the region of <a href="/wiki/South_China" title="South China">southern China</a>, going back to 1059 AD, <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, and northern <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Purdue_4-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> Unofficial records in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a> refer to lychee as far back as 2000 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-Andersen_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Andersen-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Wild trees still grow in parts of southern China and on <a href="/wiki/Hainan_Island" class="mw-redirect" title="Hainan Island">Hainan Island</a>. The fruit was used as a delicacy in the Chinese Imperial Court.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In the 1st century during the <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">Han dynasty</a>, fresh lychees were a popular <a href="/wiki/Tribute" title="Tribute">tribute</a> item, and in such demand at the Imperial Court that a special courier service with fast horses would bring the fresh fruit from Guangdong.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> There was great demand for lychee in the Song Dynasty (960-1279), according to <a href="/wiki/Cai_Xiang" title="Cai Xiang">Cai Xiang</a>, in his <i>Li chi pu</i> (Treatise on Lychees). It was also the favorite fruit of <a href="/wiki/Emperor_of_China" title="Emperor of China">Emperor</a> <a href="/wiki/Emperor_Xuanzong_of_Tang_China" class="mw-redirect" title="Emperor Xuanzong of Tang China">Li Longji (Xuanzong)</a>'s favored concubine <a href="/wiki/Yang_Guifei" title="Yang Guifei">Yang Yuhuan (Yang Guifei)</a>.  The emperor had the fruit delivered at great expense to the capital.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The lychee attracted the attention of <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Europe" title="Ethnic groups in Europe">European</a> travelers, such as the Spanish bishop, explorer, and sinologist <a href="/wiki/Juan_Gonz%C3%A1lez_de_Mendoza" title="Juan González de Mendoza">Juan González de Mendoza</a> in his <i>History of the great and mighty kingdom of China</i> (1585; English translation 1588), based on the reports of Spanish <a href="/wiki/Friar" title="Friar">friars</a> who had visited China in the 1570s gave the fruit high praise:<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1211633275">.mw-parser-output .templatequote{overflow:hidden;margin:1em 0;padding:0 32px}.mw-parser-output .templatequote .templatequotecite{line-height:1.5em;text-align:left;padding-left:1.6em;margin-top:0}</style><blockquote class="templatequote"><p>[T]hey haue a kinde of plummes, that they doo call <i>lechias</i>, that are of an exceeding gallant tast, and neuer hurteth any body, although they shoulde eate a great number of them.</p></blockquote>
<p>Later the lychee was described and introduced to the West in 1656 by <a href="/wiki/Michal_Boym" class="mw-redirect" title="Michal Boym">Michal Boym</a>, a Polish Jesuit missionary (at that time <a href="/wiki/Polish%E2%80%93Lithuanian_Commonwealth" title="Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth">Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-kaj_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-kaj-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lychee was introduced in the Pakistani region (then <a href="/wiki/British_Raj" title="British Raj">British Raj</a>) in 1932 and remained an exotic plant until the 1960s when commercial production began. The crop's production expanded from Begum Kot (<a href="/wiki/Lahore_District" title="Lahore District">Lahore District</a>) in Punjab to <a href="/wiki/Hazara_Division" title="Hazara Division">Hazara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haripur_District" title="Haripur District">Haripur</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sialkot" title="Sialkot">Sialkot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mirpur_Khas_District" title="Mirpur Khas District">Mirpur Khas</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Double_domestication">Double domestication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lychee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Double domestication"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/Genomics" title="Genomics">Genomic studies</a> indicate that the lychee resulted from double domestication by independent cultivation in two different regions of ancient China.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation_and_uses">Cultivation and uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lychee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Cultivation and uses"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Litchi_root.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Litchi_root.jpg/170px-Litchi_root.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="248" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Litchi_root.jpg/255px-Litchi_root.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Litchi_root.jpg/340px-Litchi_root.jpg 2x" data-file-width="548" data-file-height="800" /></a><figcaption>Germinating lychee seed with its main root (about 3 months old)</figcaption></figure>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-right" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Lychee_seed.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a9/Lychee_seed.jpg/220px-Lychee_seed.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a9/Lychee_seed.jpg/330px-Lychee_seed.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a9/Lychee_seed.jpg/440px-Lychee_seed.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1200" data-file-height="900" /></a><figcaption>A normal-sized seed (left) and a small-sized (Chicken tongue) seed (right)</figcaption></figure>
<p>Lychees are extensively grown in southern China, <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">Taiwan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> and the rest of tropical <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Asia" title="Southeast Asia">Southeast Asia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Indian_Subcontinent" class="mw-redirect" title="Indian Subcontinent">Indian Subcontinent</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-mitra_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mitra-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> and in <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">tropical</a> regions of many other countries.<sup id="cite_ref-Purdue_4-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mitra_19-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mitra-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> They require a tropical climate that is frost-free and is not below the temperature of −4&#160;°C (25&#160;°F).<sup id="cite_ref-Purdue_4-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mitra_19-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mitra-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Lychees require a climate with high summer heat, rainfall, and humidity, growing optimally on well-drained, slightly acidic <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soils</a> rich in organic matter and mulch.<sup id="cite_ref-Purdue_4-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mitra_19-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mitra-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Some 200 <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a> exist, with early and late maturing forms suited to warmer and cooler climates, respectively,<sup id="cite_ref-Purdue_4-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> although mainly eight cultivars are used for commerce in China.<sup id="cite_ref-mitra_19-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mitra-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> They are also grown as an <a href="/wiki/Ornamental_tree" class="mw-redirect" title="Ornamental tree">ornamental tree</a>, as well as for their fruit.<sup id="cite_ref-Purdue_4-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup> The most common way of propagating lychee is through a method called air layering or marcotting. Air-layers, or marcotts, are made by cutting a branch of a mature tree, covering the cut with a rooting medium, such as <a href="/wiki/Peat" title="Peat">peat</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sphagnum" title="Sphagnum">sphagnum moss</a>, then wrapping the medium with <a href="/wiki/Polyethylene" title="Polyethylene">polyethylene</a> film and allowing the cut to root. Once significant rooting has occurred, the marcott is cut from the branch and potted.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>According to <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">folklore</a>, a lychee tree that is not producing much fruit can be <a href="/wiki/Girdling" title="Girdling">girdled</a>, leading to more fruit production. When the central opening of trees is carried out as part of training and pruning, <a href="/wiki/Stereo_fruiting" title="Stereo fruiting">stereo fruiting</a> can be achieved for higher orchard productivity.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Lychees are commonly sold fresh in Asian markets.<sup id="cite_ref-Purdue_4-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Purdue-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-mitra_19-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mitra-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> The red rind turns dark brown when the fruit is refrigerated, but the taste isn't affected. It is also sold canned year-round. The fruit can be dried with the rind intact, at which point the flesh shrinks and darkens.<sup id="cite_ref-Oxford_11-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Oxford-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivars">Cultivars</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lychee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cultivars"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are numerous lychee <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a>, with considerable confusion regarding their naming and identification. The same cultivar grown in different climates can produce very different fruit. Cultivars can also have different synonyms in various parts of the world. Southeast Asian countries, along with Australia, use the original Chinese names for the main cultivars. India grows more than a dozen different cultivars. South Africa grows mainly the "Mauritius" cultivar. Most cultivars grown in the United States were imported from China, except for the "Groff", which was developed in the state of Hawaii.<sup id="cite_ref-Barret_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barret-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Different cultivars of lychee are popular in various growing regions and countries. In China, popular cultivars include Sanyuehong, Baitangying, Baila, Muzaffarpur, Samastipur, Shuidong, Feizixiao, Dazou, Heiye, Nuomici, Guiwei, Huaizhi, Lanzhu, and Chenzi. In Vietnam, the most popular cultivar is Vai Thieu Hai Duong. In the US, production is based on several cultivars, including Mauritius, Brewster, and Hak Ip.<sup id="cite_ref-Menzel_10-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Menzel-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup> India grows more than a dozen named cultivars, including Shahi (Highest Pulp&#160;%), Dehradun, Early Large Red, Kalkattia and Rose Scented.<sup id="cite_ref-Barret_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Barret-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Salunkhe_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Salunkhe-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>Raw lychee fruit is 82% water, 17% <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrate" title="Carbohydrate">carbohydrates</a>, 1% <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>, and contains negligible <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fat</a> (table). In a 100-gram (3.5 oz) reference amount, raw lychee fruit supplies 66 <a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">calories</a> of food energy. The raw pulp is rich in <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">vitamin C</a>, having 72&#160;mg per 100&#160;grams – an amount representing 86% of the <a href="/wiki/Daily_Value" class="mw-redirect" title="Daily Value">Daily Value</a> – but contains no other <a href="/wiki/Micronutrient" title="Micronutrient">micronutrients</a> in significant content (table).
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Phytochemicals">Phytochemicals</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lychee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Phytochemicals"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Lychees have moderate amounts of <a href="/wiki/Polyphenol" title="Polyphenol">polyphenols</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> including <a href="/wiki/Flavan-3-ol" title="Flavan-3-ol">flavan-3-ol</a> monomers and dimers as major compounds representing about 87% of total polyphenols, which declined in content during storage or <a href="/wiki/Food_browning" title="Food browning">browning</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-zhang_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zhang-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Cyanidin-3-glucoside" class="mw-redirect" title="Cyanidin-3-glucoside">Cyanidin-3-glucoside</a> represented 92% of total <a href="/wiki/Anthocyanin" title="Anthocyanin">anthocyanins</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-zhang_28-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-zhang-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Poisoning">Poisoning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lychee&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Poisoning"><span>edit</span></a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1962, it was found that lychee seeds contained <a href="/wiki/Methylene_cyclopropyl_acetic_acid" title="Methylene cyclopropyl acetic acid">methylenecyclopropylglycine</a> (MCPG), a <a href="/wiki/Homology_(biology)" title="Homology (biology)">homologue</a> of <a href="/wiki/Hypoglycin_A" title="Hypoglycin A">hypoglycin A</a>, which caused <a href="/wiki/Hypoglycemia" title="Hypoglycemia">hypoglycemia</a> in human and animal studies.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
Since the end of the 1990s, unexplained outbreaks of <a href="/wiki/Encephalopathy" title="Encephalopathy">encephalopathy</a> occurred, appearing to affect only children in India<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup> (where it is called <i>chamki bukhar</i>),<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup> and northern <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">Vietnam</a> (where it was called Ac Mong encephalitis after the Vietnamese word for <a href="/wiki/Nightmare" title="Nightmare">nightmare</a>) during the lychee harvest season from May to June.<sup id="cite_ref-paireua_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-paireua-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A 2013 investigation by the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Centers_for_Disease_Control_and_Prevention" title="Centers for Disease Control and Prevention">Centers for Disease Control and Prevention</a> (CDC), in India, showed that cases were linked to the consumption of lychee fruit,<sup id="cite_ref-CDC_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDC-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup> causing a noninflammatory <a href="/wiki/Encephalopathy" title="Encephalopathy">encephalopathy</a> that mimicked symptoms of <a href="/wiki/Jamaican_vomiting_sickness" title="Jamaican vomiting sickness">Jamaican vomiting sickness</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup> Because low blood sugar (<a href="/wiki/Hypoglycemia" title="Hypoglycemia">hypoglycemia</a>) of less than 70&#160;mg/dL in the <a href="/wiki/Undernourished" class="mw-redirect" title="Undernourished">undernourished</a> children on admission was common, and associated with a poorer outcome (44% of all cases were fatal) the CDC identified the illness as a <a href="/wiki/Hypoglycemia" title="Hypoglycemia">hypoglycemic</a> encephalopathy.<sup id="cite_ref-CDC_34-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDC-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The investigation linked the illness to hypoglycin A and MCPG toxicity, and to <a href="/wiki/Malnutrition" title="Malnutrition">malnourished</a> children eating lychees (particularly unripe ones) on an empty stomach.<sup id="cite_ref-lancet2_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lancet2-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The CDC report recommended that parents ensure their children limit lychee consumption and have an evening meal, elevating blood glucose levels that may be sufficient to deter illness.<sup id="cite_ref-CDC_34-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CDC-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-nyt_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Earlier studies had incorrectly concluded that transmission may occur from direct contact with lychees contaminated by <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">bat</a> <a href="/wiki/Saliva" title="Saliva">saliva</a>, urine, or <a href="/wiki/Guano" title="Guano">guano</a> or with other <a href="/wiki/Vector_(epidemiology)" class="mw-redirect" title="Vector (epidemiology)">vectors</a>, such as insects found in lychee trees or <a href="/wiki/Sand_flies" class="mw-redirect" title="Sand flies">sand flies</a>, as in the case of <a href="/wiki/Chandipura_virus" class="mw-redirect" title="Chandipura virus">Chandipura virus</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-paireua_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-paireua-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup> A 2017 study found that pesticides used in the plantations could be responsible for the encephalitis and deaths of young children in <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">Bangladesh</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
