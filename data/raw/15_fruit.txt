<p>A <b>cherry</b> is the <a href="/wiki/Fruit" title="Fruit">fruit</a> of many plants of the genus <i><a href="/wiki/Prunus" title="Prunus">Prunus</a></i>, and is a fleshy <a href="/wiki/Drupe" title="Drupe">drupe</a> (stone fruit).
</p><p>Commercial cherries are obtained from <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a> of several species, such as the sweet <i><a href="/wiki/Prunus_avium" title="Prunus avium">Prunus avium</a></i> and the sour <i><a href="/wiki/Prunus_cerasus" title="Prunus cerasus">Prunus cerasus</a></i>. The name 'cherry' also refers to the cherry tree and its wood, and is sometimes applied to <a href="/wiki/Almond" title="Almond">almonds</a> and visually similar flowering trees in the genus <i>Prunus</i>, as in "<a href="/wiki/Ornamental_plant" title="Ornamental plant">ornamental</a> cherry" or "<a href="/wiki/Cherry_blossom" title="Cherry blossom">cherry blossom</a>." <b>Wild cherry</b> may refer to any of the cherry species growing outside cultivation, although <i>Prunus avium</i> is often referred to specifically by the name "wild cherry" in the British Isles.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span></h2>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Sara_Ploos_van_Amstel_-_The_Cherry_Seller_-_watercolor_after_Jacob_Ochtervelt.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Sara_Ploos_van_Amstel_-_The_Cherry_Seller_-_watercolor_after_Jacob_Ochtervelt.jpg/170px-Sara_Ploos_van_Amstel_-_The_Cherry_Seller_-_watercolor_after_Jacob_Ochtervelt.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="224" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Sara_Ploos_van_Amstel_-_The_Cherry_Seller_-_watercolor_after_Jacob_Ochtervelt.jpg/255px-Sara_Ploos_van_Amstel_-_The_Cherry_Seller_-_watercolor_after_Jacob_Ochtervelt.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Sara_Ploos_van_Amstel_-_The_Cherry_Seller_-_watercolor_after_Jacob_Ochtervelt.jpg/340px-Sara_Ploos_van_Amstel_-_The_Cherry_Seller_-_watercolor_after_Jacob_Ochtervelt.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1851" data-file-height="2439" /></a><figcaption>The Cherry Seller, by <a href="/wiki/Sara_Troost" title="Sara Troost">Sara Troost</a> (18th Century Netherlands)</figcaption></figure>
<p>The English word cherry derives from <a href="/wiki/Old_Northern_French" class="mw-redirect" title="Old Northern French">Old Northern French</a> or <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">Norman</a> <i>cherise</i> from the Latin <i>cerasum</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup> referring to an ancient Greek region, Kerasous (Κερασοῦς) near <a href="/wiki/Giresun" title="Giresun">Giresun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, from which cherries were first thought to be exported to Europe.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The word "cherry" is also used for some species that bear fruits with similar size and shape even though they are not in the same <i>Prunus</i> genus; some of these species include the "<a href="/wiki/Muntingia" title="Muntingia">Jamaican cherry</a>" (<i>Muntingia calabura</i>) and the "<a href="/wiki/Mimusops_elengi" title="Mimusops elengi">Spanish cherry</a>" (<i>Mimusops elengi</i>).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Antiquity">Antiquity</span></h2>
<p>The indigenous range of the <a href="/wiki/Prunus_avium" title="Prunus avium">sweet cherry</a> extends through most of Europe, western Asia, and parts of northern Africa, and the fruit has been consumed throughout its range since prehistoric times. A cultivated cherry is recorded as having been brought to Rome by <a href="/wiki/Lucullus" title="Lucullus">Lucius Licinius Lucullus</a> from northeastern <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">Anatolia</a>, also known as the <a href="/wiki/Pontus_(region)" title="Pontus (region)">Pontus</a> region, in 72 BC.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cherries were introduced into England at <a href="/wiki/Teynham" title="Teynham">Teynham</a>, near <a href="/wiki/Sittingbourne" title="Sittingbourne">Sittingbourne</a> in <a href="/wiki/Kent" title="Kent">Kent</a>, by order of <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry VIII of England">Henry VIII</a>, who had tasted them in <a href="/wiki/Flanders" title="Flanders">Flanders</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Cherries, along with many other fruiting trees and plants, probably first arrived in North America around 1606 in the <a href="/wiki/New_France" title="New France">New France</a> colony of <a href="/wiki/Port-Royal_(Acadia)" title="Port-Royal (Acadia)">Port Royal</a>, which is modern-day <a href="/wiki/Annapolis_Royal" title="Annapolis Royal">Annapolis Royal</a>, Nova Scotia. Richard Guthrie described in 1629, the "fruitful valley adorned with...great variety of fruit trees, chestnuts, pears, apples, cherries, plums and all other fruits."<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Cultivation">Cultivation</span></h2>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">"Cherry orchard" redirects here. For other uses, see <a href="/wiki/Cherry_Orchard_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" title="Cherry Orchard (disambiguation)">Cherry Orchard (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Salvatore_Postiglione_Kirschenzeit.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Salvatore_Postiglione_Kirschenzeit.jpg/170px-Salvatore_Postiglione_Kirschenzeit.jpg" decoding="async" width="170" height="271" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Salvatore_Postiglione_Kirschenzeit.jpg/255px-Salvatore_Postiglione_Kirschenzeit.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Salvatore_Postiglione_Kirschenzeit.jpg/340px-Salvatore_Postiglione_Kirschenzeit.jpg 2x" data-file-width="3156" data-file-height="5040" /></a><figcaption><a href="/wiki/Salvatore_Postiglione" title="Salvatore Postiglione">Salvatore Postiglione</a> <i>Cherry time</i></figcaption></figure>
<p>The cultivated forms are of the species <a href="/wiki/Prunus_avium" title="Prunus avium">sweet cherry</a> (<i>P. avium</i>) to which most cherry <a href="/wiki/Cultivar" title="Cultivar">cultivars</a> belong, and the <a href="/wiki/Sour_cherry" class="mw-redirect" title="Sour cherry">sour cherry</a> (<i>P. cerasus</i>), which is used mainly for cooking. Both species originate in Europe and western Asia; they usually do not <a href="/wiki/Cross-pollinate" class="mw-redirect" title="Cross-pollinate">cross-pollinate</a>. Some other species, although having edible fruit, are not grown extensively for consumption, except in northern regions where the two main species will not grow. Irrigation, spraying, labor, and their propensity to damage from rain and hail make cherries relatively expensive. Nonetheless, demand is high for the fruit. In commercial production, sour cherries, as well as sweet cherries sometimes, are harvested by using a mechanized "shaker."<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> Hand picking is also widely used for sweet as well as sour cherries to harvest the fruit to avoid damage to both fruit and trees.
</p><p>Common rootstocks include Mazzard, Mahaleb, Colt, and Gisela Series, a dwarfing rootstock that produces trees significantly smaller than others, only 8 to 10 feet (2.5 to 3 meters) tall.<sup id="cite_ref-ingels_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ingels-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup> Sour cherries require no <a href="/wiki/Pollenizer" title="Pollenizer">pollenizer</a>, while few sweet varieties are self-fertile.<sup id="cite_ref-ingels_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ingels-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A cherry tree will take three to four years once it is planted in the orchard to produce its first crop of fruit, and seven years to attain full maturity.<sup id="cite_ref-ucd_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ucd-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Growing_season">Growing season</span></h3>
<p>Like most temperate-latitude trees, cherry trees require a certain number of chilling hours each year to break dormancy and bloom and produce fruit. The number of chilling hours required depends on the variety. Because of this cold-weather requirement, no members of the genus <i>Prunus</i> can grow in tropical climates. (See "production" section for more information on <a href="/wiki/Chilling_requirement" title="Chilling requirement">chilling requirements</a>)
</p><p>Cherries have a short growing season and can grow in most <a href="/wiki/Temperateness" class="mw-redirect" title="Temperateness">temperate</a> latitudes.<sup id="cite_ref-ucd_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ucd-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Cherries blossom in April (in the Northern Hemisphere) and the peak season for the cherry harvest is in the summer. In <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">southern Europe</a> in June, in <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a> in June, in <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a> in mid-July, and in southern <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a>) in June to mid-August. In many parts of North America, they are among the first tree fruits to flower and ripen in mid-Spring.
</p><p>In the Southern Hemisphere, cherries are usually at their peak in late December and are widely associated with <a href="/wiki/Christmas" title="Christmas">Christmas</a>. 'Burlat' is an early variety which ripens during the beginning of December, 'Lapins' ripens near the end of December, and 'Sweetheart' finish slightly later.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pests_and_diseases">Pests and diseases</span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Brunatna_zgnilizna_drzew_pestkowych.jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Brunatna_zgnilizna_drzew_pestkowych.jpg/220px-Brunatna_zgnilizna_drzew_pestkowych.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="165" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Brunatna_zgnilizna_drzew_pestkowych.jpg/330px-Brunatna_zgnilizna_drzew_pestkowych.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Brunatna_zgnilizna_drzew_pestkowych.jpg/440px-Brunatna_zgnilizna_drzew_pestkowych.jpg 2x" data-file-width="2832" data-file-height="2128" /></a><figcaption>Cherries with <i><a href="/wiki/Monilinia_laxa" title="Monilinia laxa">Monilinia laxa</a></i></figcaption></figure>
<p>Generally, the cherry can be a difficult fruit tree to grow and keep alive.<sup id="cite_ref-ingels_12-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ingels-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>  In Europe, the first visible pest in the growing season soon after blossom (in April in western Europe) usually is the <a href="/wiki/Myzus_cerasi" title="Myzus cerasi">black cherry aphid</a> ("cherry blackfly," <i>Myzus cerasi</i>), which causes leaves at the tips of branches to curl, with the blackfly colonies exuding a sticky secretion which promotes fungal growth on the leaves and fruit. At the fruiting stage in June/July (Europe), the <a href="/wiki/Rhagoletis_cerasi" title="Rhagoletis cerasi">cherry fruit fly</a> (<i>Rhagoletis cingulata</i> and <i>Rhagoletis cerasi</i>) lays its eggs in the immature fruit, whereafter its larvae feed on the cherry flesh and exit through a small hole (about 1&#160;mm diameter), which in turn is the entry point for fungal infection of the cherry fruit after rainfall.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> In addition, cherry trees are susceptible to bacterial <a href="/wiki/Canker" title="Canker">canker</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cytospora" title="Cytospora">cytospora</a> canker, <a href="/wiki/Monilinia_fructicola" title="Monilinia fructicola">brown rot of the fruit</a>, <a href="/wiki/Root_rot" title="Root rot">root rot</a> from overly wet soil, crown rot, and several viruses.<sup id="cite_ref-ingels_12-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ingels-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In 2020, world production of sweet cherries was 2.61 million <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">tonnes</a>, with <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a> producing 28% of this total. Other major producers of sweet cherries were the United States and <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a>. World production of sour cherries in 2020 was 1.48 million tonnes, led by <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, Ukraine and <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">Serbia</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_East">Middle East</span></h3>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Cherry_trees_in_Tehran..jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Cherry_trees_in_Tehran..jpg/220px-Cherry_trees_in_Tehran..jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="124" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Cherry_trees_in_Tehran..jpg/330px-Cherry_trees_in_Tehran..jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Cherry_trees_in_Tehran..jpg/440px-Cherry_trees_in_Tehran..jpg 2x" data-file-width="2592" data-file-height="1456" /></a><figcaption>Ripe sweet cherries in <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">Tehran</a></figcaption></figure>
<p>Major commercial cherry orchards in West Asia are in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">Iran</a>, <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">Syria</a>, <a href="/wiki/Azerbaijan" title="Azerbaijan">Azerbaijan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">Lebanon</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a>.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span></h3>
<p>Major commercial cherry orchards in Europe are in <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italy</a>, <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> and other Mediterranean regions, and to a smaller extent in the <a href="/wiki/Baltic_States" class="mw-redirect" title="Baltic States">Baltic States</a> and southern <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>.
</p><p>In <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> since the 1920s, the first cherries of the season come in April/May from the region of <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9ret" title="Céret">Céret</a> (<a href="/wiki/Pyr%C3%A9n%C3%A9es-Orientales" title="Pyrénées-Orientales">Pyrénées-Orientales</a>),<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> where the local producers send, as a tradition since 1932, the first crate of cherries to the <a href="/wiki/President_of_France" title="President of France">president of the Republic</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span></h3>
<link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1033289096"><div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">See also: <a href="/wiki/Cherry_production_in_Michigan" title="Cherry production in Michigan">Cherry production in Michigan</a></div>
<figure class="mw-default-size mw-halign-left" typeof="mw:File/Thumb"><a href="/wiki/File:Cherry_season_(48216568192).jpg" class="mw-file-description"><img src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Cherry_season_%2848216568192%29.jpg/220px-Cherry_season_%2848216568192%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="147" class="mw-file-element" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Cherry_season_%2848216568192%29.jpg/330px-Cherry_season_%2848216568192%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Cherry_season_%2848216568192%29.jpg/440px-Cherry_season_%2848216568192%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1920" data-file-height="1280" /></a><figcaption>Fresh Michigan cherries in a basket</figcaption></figure>
<p>In the United States, most sweet cherries are grown in <a href="/wiki/Washington_(state)" title="Washington (state)">Washington</a>, <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">California</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oregon" title="Oregon">Oregon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">Wisconsin</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Michigan" title="Michigan">Michigan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NASS_43-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASS-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Important sweet cherry cultivars include <a href="/wiki/Bing_cherry" title="Bing cherry">Bing</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ulster_cherry" title="Ulster cherry">Ulster</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rainier_cherry" title="Rainier cherry">Rainier</a>, Brooks, Tulare, King, and Sweetheart.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Both Oregon and Michigan provide light-colored 'Royal Ann' ('Napoleon'; alternately 'Queen Anne') cherries for the <a href="/wiki/Maraschino_cherry" title="Maraschino cherry">maraschino cherry</a> process. Most sour (also called tart) cherries are grown in Michigan, followed by <a href="/wiki/Utah" title="Utah">Utah</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">New York</a>, and Washington.<sup id="cite_ref-NASS_43-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NASS-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Sour cherries include 'Nanking' and <a href="/wiki/Evans_cherry" class="mw-redirect" title="Evans cherry">'Evans</a>.' <a href="/wiki/Traverse_City,_Michigan" title="Traverse City, Michigan">Traverse City, Michigan</a> is called the "Cherry Capital of the World",<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> hosting a <a href="/wiki/National_Cherry_Festival" title="National Cherry Festival">National Cherry Festival</a> and making the world's largest <a href="/wiki/Cherry_pie" title="Cherry pie">cherry pie</a>. The specific region of northern Michigan known for tart cherry production is referred to as the "Traverse Bay" region.
</p><p>Most cherry varieties have a chilling requirement of 800 or more hours, meaning that in order to break dormancy, blossom, and set fruit, the winter season needs to have at least 800 hours where the temperature is below 7&#160;°C (45&#160;°F). "Low chill" varieties requiring 300 hours or less are Minnie Royal and Royal Lee, requiring <a href="/wiki/Pollination" title="Pollination">cross-pollinization</a>, whereas the cultivar, Royal Crimson, is self-fertile.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup>  These varieties extend the range of cultivation of cherries to the mild winter areas of southern US. This is a boon to California producers of sweet cherries, as California is the second largest producer of sweet cherries in the US.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Native and non-native sweet cherries grow well in Canada's <a href="/wiki/Provinces" class="mw-redirect" title="Provinces">provinces</a> of <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">Ontario</a> and <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a> where an annual cherry festival has been celebrated for seven consecutive decades in the <a href="/wiki/Okanagan_Valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Okanagan Valley">Okanagan Valley</a> town of <a href="/wiki/Osoyoos" title="Osoyoos">Osoyoos</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-48" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup> In addition to the Okanagan, other British Columbia cherry growing regions are the <a href="/wiki/Similkameen_Valley" class="mw-redirect" title="Similkameen Valley">Similkameen Valley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kootenays" title="Kootenays">Kootenay Valley</a>, all three regions together producing 5.5 million kg annually or 60% of total Canadian output.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> Sweet cherry varieties in British Columbia include 'Rainier', 'Van', 'Chelan', 'Lapins', 'Sweetheart', 'Skeena', 'Staccato', 'Christalina' and 'Bing.'
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">Australia</span></h3>
<p>In Australia, cherries are grown in all the states except for the Northern Territory.  The major producing regions are located in the temperate areas within New South Wales, Victoria, South Australia and Tasmania.  Western Australia has limited production in the elevated parts in the southwest of the state.  Key production areas include <a href="/wiki/Young,_New_South_Wales" title="Young, New South Wales">Young</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orange,_New_South_Wales" title="Orange, New South Wales">Orange</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bathurst,_New_South_Wales" title="Bathurst, New South Wales">Bathurst</a> in <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wandin" class="mw-redirect" title="Wandin">Wandin</a>, the Goulburn and Murray valley areas in <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" class="mw-redirect" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Adelaide_Hills" title="Adelaide Hills">Adelaide Hills</a> region in <a href="/wiki/South_Australia" title="South Australia">South Australia</a>, and the Huon and Derwent Valleys in <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a>.
</p><p>Key commercial varieties in order of seasonality include 'Empress', 'Merchant', 'Supreme', 'Ron's seedling', 'Chelan', 'Ulster', 'Van', 'Bing', 'Stella', 'Nordwunder', 'Lapins', 'Simone', 'Regina', 'Kordia' and 'Sweetheart'.  New varieties are being introduced, including the late season 'Staccato' and early season 'Sequoia'.  The Australian Cherry Breeding program is developing a series of new varieties which are under testing evaluation.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>The New South Wales town of <a href="/wiki/Young,_New_South_Wales" title="Young, New South Wales">Young</a> is called the "Cherry Capital of Australia" and hosts the National Cherry Festival.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nutritional_value">Nutritional value</span></h2>
<p>Raw sweet cherries are 82% water, 16% <a href="/wiki/Carbohydrates" class="mw-redirect" title="Carbohydrates">carbohydrates</a>, 1% <a href="/wiki/Protein" title="Protein">protein</a>, and negligible in <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">fat</a> (table). As raw fruit, sweet cherries provide little nutrient content per 100 g serving, as only <a href="/wiki/Dietary_fiber" title="Dietary fiber">dietary fiber</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vitamin_C" title="Vitamin C">vitamin C</a> are present in moderate content, while other <a href="/wiki/Vitamin" title="Vitamin">vitamins</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mineral_(nutrient)" title="Mineral (nutrient)">dietary minerals</a> each supply less than 10% of the <a href="/wiki/Daily_Value" class="mw-redirect" title="Daily Value">Daily Value</a> (DV) per serving, respectively (table).<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Compared to sweet cherries, raw <a href="/wiki/Prunus_cerasus" title="Prunus cerasus">sour cherries</a> contain 50% more vitamin C per 100 g (12% DV) and about 20 times more vitamin A (8% DV), <a href="/wiki/Beta-Carotene" class="mw-redirect" title="Beta-Carotene">beta-Carotene</a> in particular (table).<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Health_risks">Health risks</span></h3>
<p>The cherry kernels, accessible by chewing or breaking the hard-shelled cherry <a href="/wiki/Pyrena" title="Pyrena">pits</a>, contain <a href="/wiki/Amygdalin" title="Amygdalin">amygdalin</a>, a chemical that releases the toxic compound <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide" title="Hydrogen cyanide">hydrogen cyanide</a> when ingested. The amount of amygdalin in each cherry varies widely, and symptoms would show only after eating several crushed pits (3–4 of the <a href="/wiki/Prunus_cerasus" title="Prunus cerasus">Morello</a> variety or 7–9 of the red or black varieties). Swallowing the pits whole normally causes no complications.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
